SAFEGUARDING

Safeguarding is essential because it protects the wellbeing, dignity, and safety of individuals, ensuring that everyone, especially those who are vulnerable, are free from harm, abuse, and neglect.

What is Safeguarding? Signs to Watch For Action Steps

What is Safeguarding?

Creating a safe and welcoming environment, where everyone is respected and valued, is at the heart of safeguarding.

It's about making sure your organisation is run in a way that actively prevents harm, harassment, bullying, abuse and neglect. It's also about being ready to respond safely and well if there is a problem.

Everyone in the organisation has a role to play in safeguarding. It should become part of your day to day activities.

Every organisation that delivers charitable activities has a duty to safeguard volunteers, staff members, participants and donors.

Signs to Watch For

Unexplained Injuries: The presence of bruising, cuts, burns, or fractures with no consistent or believable explanation.

Changes in Behaviour: The person becoming withdrawn, anxious, aggressive, or subdued, especially around a particular individual.

Poor Physical Condition: An unkempt appearance, poor personal hygiene, inappropriate clothing for the weather, or signs of malnutrition/dehydration.

Financial Irregularities: Unexplained lack of money, missing possessions, or confusion regarding their finances.

Fear or Reluctance: A reluctance to be alone with specific staff members, or appearing fearful of external intervention (e.g., medical professionals or family members).

Inappropriate Knowledge or Behaviour: Children or vulnerable adults exhibiting overtly sexual behaviour or using sexualised language beyond their expected understanding.

Action Steps

  • Recognise

    Recognising that the person is vulnerable and then spotting the signs of abuse is often the first step in safeguarding adults and children effectively. This step sets the stage for taking further action in safeguarding adults and children.

  • Respond

    Responding in an appropriate manner is vital, as this ensures that the situation is acknowledged and addressed promptly, preventing further harm. Handle the situation calmly with great care and caution, as some people may not be willing to talk about their situation. Ask open questions to encourage a response

  • Report

    Reporting is a crucial step in escalating concerns and should be acted upon immediately. Know who your closest safeguarding lead is. Report any concerns that may have arisen.to that designated person or team responsible, The designated person or team will then take responsibility for dealing with the matter professionally.

  • Record

    Record an incident as soon as possible. Record an incident in the best detail, including who is involved, dates and times. Take precise and comprehensive notes of the situation to assist in your report and relay these where necessary. In the notes, use the who, what, why, when and where of safeguarding. Keeping detailed records ensures there is an accurate account of what happened, which can be invaluable during an investigation.

  • Refer

    The report should be referred to the appropriate authorities to continue the investigation. Referring is the final step of safeguarding and ensures the issue is handled by professionals equipped to investigate and resolve it. Referrals ensure that the safeguarding concern is addressed effectively and promptly, minimizing potential harm to the individual. If you believe the safeguarding risk to be more urgent, such as a suspicion that somebody is under immediate or severe threat, you should contact the relevant authority immediately, whether this is the police, fire service or ambulance crew.
